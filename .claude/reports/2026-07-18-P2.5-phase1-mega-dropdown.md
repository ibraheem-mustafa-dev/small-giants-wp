**Doc:** P2.5 Phase-1 research input — general web / UI-UX navigation best practice **beyond WordPress**
**Date:** 2026-07-18
**Author:** navigation-UX research pass
**Scope:** DESKTOP rich navigation only — mega-menus + dropdowns. This is the ONE missing input; WP core baseline and WP-theme competitors (Kadence/Spectra/GenerateBlocks/Elementor/Bricks) are covered by other inputs and deliberately NOT re-covered here.

Each entry has: **(1) Pattern/standard · (2) Plain-English what-it-is · (3) Source URL · (4) Why it matters · (5) Tier** (MUST-HAVE / SHOULD-HAVE / NICE-TO-HAVE / EMERGING for a best-in-class 2026 nav).

## Section A — Trigger models & open/close timing

| # | Pattern / standard | What it is (plain English) | Source | Why it matters (problem solved) | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A1 | **Hover-intent delay before OPEN (~0.5s stationary)** | Don't open a hover panel the instant the cursor touches a bar item. Wait until the pointer has rested ~0.5s, then reveal within ~0.1s. | NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ · NN/g "Timing Guidelines for Exposing Hidden Content" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/timing-exposing-content/ | Stops menus firing while the user is just moving the cursor across the bar to somewhere else. NN/g: only after 0.5s resting can you assume the user actually wants that panel. **[fetch-verified: 0.5s wait, 0.1s show]** | MUST-HAVE |
| A2 | **Hover delay of 300–500ms (the "flicker" fix)** | E-commerce benchmark equivalent of A1: a 300–500ms delay eliminates most accidental triggering; 60% of sites get this wrong. | Baymard "Provide a Hover Delay of 300–500 MS…" https://baymard.com/blog/dropdown-menu-flickering-issue | Without it, menus flicker open/closed as the user scans; testers called nav "flimsy"/"disorienting" and abandoned the nav for search. 74% of desktop e-com sites use hover dropdowns, so this is the dominant failure surface. **[fetch-verified: 300–500ms, 60%, 74%]** | MUST-HAVE |
| A3 | **Close delay before HIDE (~0.5s grace)** | When the cursor leaves the trigger/panel, wait a beat (~0.5s) before closing, then hide within ~0.1s. | NN/g "Timing Guidelines…" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/timing-exposing-content/ | Prevents the panel vanishing when the user briefly crosses a gap or overshoots — a top cause of "the menu ran away from me". **[fetch-verified]** | MUST-HAVE |
| A4 | **The diagonal / "whack-a-menu" problem** | Moving the cursor diagonally from a parent item toward the panel crosses OFF the trigger, so a naive implementation closes the panel before the user reaches it. | NN/g "Timing Guidelines…" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/timing-exposing-content/ · Ben Kamens "Breaking down Amazon's mega dropdown" https://bjk5.com/post/44698559168/breaking-down-amazons-mega-dropdown | This is THE classic mega-menu failure. Any rich nav with a submenu offset from its trigger must solve it or feel broken. | MUST-HAVE (must be solved) |
| A5 | **Safe-triangle / prediction-cone hover path** | Track the cursor; while it moves inside a triangle drawn from the current cursor to the panel's two far corners, keep the panel open regardless of which item it technically crosses. First invented by Tog for Apple, 1986; popularised by Amazon/Ben Kamens 2013. | Smashing "Better Context Menus With Safe Triangles" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2023/08/better-context-menus-safe-triangles/ · CSS-Tricks "Dropdown Menus with More Forgiving Mouse Movement Paths" https://css-tricks.com/dropdown-menus-with-more-forgiving-mouse-movement-paths/ · Ambient.Impact "Amazon's menu prediction cone" https://ambientimpact.com/web/snippets/amazons-menu-prediction-cone | Solves A4 WITHOUT a sluggish delay — the panel stays snappy AND forgiving. Superior to delay-only because it reacts to *intent* (movement direction), not arbitrary time. Implementable in vanilla JS via a `mousemove` listener + geometry (Smashing uses an SVG path with `pointer-events`). | SHOULD-HAVE (MUST-HAVE if panels are offset from triggers) |
| A6 | **Curved / "aim-aware" safe areas** | Refinement of A5 using a curved path instead of a straight triangle, better fitting certain panel shapes (e.g. Needle's approach). | Smashing "Better Context Menus With Safe Triangles" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2023/08/better-context-menus-safe-triangles/ | Edge-case polish for large/irregular panels where a straight triangle over- or under-shoots. | EMERGING |
| A7 | **Click-to-open trigger (accessibility + touch parity)** | Open the panel on click/Enter rather than hover. A click translates across mouse, trackpad, keyboard and touchscreen; hover does not. | Toward "Breaking up with hover" https://toward.studio/latest/breaking-up-with-hover-navigation-menus-for-websites · Chemistry Agency "The click vs. hover debate" https://www.chemistryagency.com/insights/navigational-design-the-click-vs-hover-debate/ | Hover-only excludes keyboard, motor-impaired and screen-magnifier users, and is unreliable on the ~1-in-3 touchscreen laptops. WCAG 2.2 guidance leans toward click for important content. (29% touchscreen-laptop figure is **[search-surfaced]** from Toward — treat as illustrative.) | MUST-HAVE (as an available/keyboard path) |
| A8 | **Hybrid: hover-to-open, click-also-opens, click parent still navigates** | Best-in-class desktop pattern: hover reveals (with A1/A2 intent delay), but the top-level item is also a real link/button so keyboard + click + touch all work. | NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ · Feldspar "Hover vs Click Navigation" https://feldsparcreative.com/blog/hover-vs-click-navigation-menus/ | Gives fast mouse users the hover affordance without stranding everyone else — the pattern Stripe, Apple and most exemplars actually ship. | MUST-HAVE |
| A9 | **WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.13 "Content on Hover or Focus" (Dismissible / Hoverable / Persistent)** | Any content shown on hover/focus (a mega-panel is exactly this) must be: dismissible without moving the pointer (Esc), hoverable (you can move onto it without it vanishing), and persistent (stays until dismissed / hover-away / no-longer-valid). | W3C Understanding SC 1.4.13 https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/content-on-hover-or-focus.html | This is a formal WCAG 2.1 AA criterion — the project's stated baseline. A hover mega-menu that closes when you try to move onto it FAILS AA. Directly forces A3 + Esc-to-close. | MUST-HAVE |

## Section B — Mega-panel information architecture & layout

| # | Pattern / standard | What it is (plain English) | Source | Why it matters | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| B1 | **Column grouping into related, ordered sets** | Chunk links into labelled groups (ideally derived from a card-sort), keep medium granularity, and ORDER groups by workflow or importance — most-used top-left. | NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ | Turns a wall of links into a scannable structure. Ungrouped mega-panels read as "randomness" and users give up. **[fetch-verified: card-sort, medium granularity, top-left ordering]** | MUST-HAVE |
| B2 | **Group headings (and optional icons) per column** | Each column gets a clear heading; icons may aid recognition but never replace a text label. Practical caps: ~7–9 links per group, ~3–5 groups per panel. | LogRocket "Here's how I'd design a mega menu" https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/mega-menu-design-examples/ · NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ | Headings create the visual hierarchy that makes the panel skimmable; the caps stop it becoming an overwhelming dump. (7–9 / 3–5 figures are LogRocket's practitioner guidance, **[search-surfaced]**.) | SHOULD-HAVE |
| B3 | **Show each choice only once** | Never duplicate the same link in two groups of the same panel. | NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ | Duplicates make users wonder whether the two are the same or different, and bloat the panel. **[fetch-verified]** | MUST-HAVE |
| B4 | **Vertical scan order (never horizontal alphabetical)** | Lay lists to be read top-to-bottom within a column. Don't run an A–Z (or any) sequence left-to-right across columns. | NN/g "Mega Menus Gone Wrong" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-gone-wrong/ | People scan columns vertically; a horizontally-ordered sequence makes the ordering invisible and the panel feel random. **[fetch-verified: Hulu example]** | MUST-HAVE |
| B5 | **Imagery / thumbnails that carry meaning** | Use images to help users pick the right option (product shots, category thumbnails) — but only when they communicate; decorative filler is an abuse of the space. | NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ · NN/g "Mega Menus Gone Wrong" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-gone-wrong/ · Baymard homepage & category research https://baymard.com/research/homepage-and-category-usability | Thumbnails guide especially when the label differs from the user's word (user wants "jackets", site says "outerwear"). A generic stock image with no communicative value actively wastes the panel. **[fetch-verified: Moleskine helps / USA.gov generic-image abuse]** | SHOULD-HAVE |
| B6 | **Featured / promoted item within the panel** | A visually distinct slot (highlighted item, promo card, "new/featured") inside the panel or on the bar itself. | NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ (imagery/promotion) · LogRocket mega-menu examples https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/mega-menu-design-examples/ | Directly maps to the two target menus (restaurant "featured" bar item; Indus "split-with-aside-CTA" panel). Lets marketing surface a hero destination without breaking the group structure. | SHOULD-HAVE |
| B7 | **In-panel CTA / aside** | A call-to-action block (book, enquire, "see the range") docked in the panel, often as a right-hand aside column. | LogRocket "Here's how I'd design a mega menu" https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/mega-menu-design-examples/ · Builder.io Stripe menu teardown https://www.builder.io/blog/stripe-menus | The Indus "split-with-aside-CTA" layout is exactly this. Converts browse-intent into action inside the nav. | SHOULD-HAVE |
| B8 | **"View all" / parent-category link** | Every group (and the parent) offers an explicit link to the full listing, so the panel is a shortcut, not a cage. | Baymard "Have a 'View All' Option… at Each Level" https://baymard.com/blog/mobile-main-nav-view-all | Users often want the broad list, not a curated subset; without "view all" the panel can hide the real destination. (Baymard's study is framed for mobile nav but the affordance principle applies to desktop panels.) | SHOULD-HAVE |
| B9 | **Categories are the top-level items (don't bury them under "Shop/Products")** | Put the real navigable categories on the bar / at the top of the IA rather than nesting everything under a generic label. | Baymard "Make Product Categories the Top-Level Navigation Items…" https://baymard.com/blog/main-navigation-product-categories | Burying categories under "Shop" led testers to abandon the main nav. Keeps information scent high. (Baymard measured this on mobile — 33% get it wrong — but it's an IA principle. **[search-surfaced: 33%]**.) | SHOULD-HAVE |
| B10 | **Threshold: when a mega beats a simple dropdown** | Use a simple single-column dropdown for a short, flat list (fewer options = lower cognitive load). Escalate to a two-dimensional mega-panel only when there are many options, sub-levels, or a need for imagery/description. | IxDF "What are Mega Menus?" https://ixdf.org/literature/topics/mega-menus · NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ | Prevents over-building: a mega-panel for 4 links is intimidating and slower than a plain dropdown. Directly informs the framework's "simple dropdown vs mega" block decision (Indus has 3 simple + 1 mega). | MUST-HAVE (as a decision rule) |
| B11 | **Descriptions under links (information scent)** | Short descriptive sub-text beneath a link where the label alone is ambiguous. | NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ (descriptive labels / info scent) · LogRocket mega-menu examples https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/mega-menu-design-examples/ | Raises information scent for feature/solution menus (common in B2B/SaaS like the Indus "info-box" layout). | NICE-TO-HAVE |
| B12 | **Don't cover the whole screen** | A desktop mega-panel is an ephemeral overlay — keep it to the content it needs; don't take over the entire viewport. | NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ | A full-screen takeover feels heavy, disorients, and loses the "sits on top, dismiss and continue" mental model. **[fetch-verified]** | MUST-HAVE |
| B13 | **Multiple panel layouts driven by content type** | Support distinct panel templates — photo-grid, split-with-aside-CTA, logo-grid, info-box, plain link-columns — chosen per menu, not one rigid shape. | LogRocket mega-menu examples https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/mega-menu-design-examples/ · IxDF "What are Mega Menus?" https://ixdf.org/literature/topics/mega-menus | This is the Indus Foods requirement (FIVE distinct rich layouts). Exemplars ship several templates; a single hard-coded grid can't reproduce best-in-class navs. | SHOULD-HAVE |

## Section C — Signalling, affordances & wayfinding

| # | Pattern / standard | What it is (plain English) | Source | Why it matters | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | **Caret/chevron affordance on expandable items** | A downward caret (▾) signals "expand to see more"; a right chevron (›) implies "go deeper". Put one next to any item that opens a panel. | Design Monks "Caret vs Chevron" https://www.designmonks.co/blog/caret-vs-chevron · Orange a11y "Dropdown menu" https://a11y-guidelines.orange.com/en/articles/dropdown-menu/ | Without the glyph, users don't know an item is expandable vs a plain link — they mis-click or never discover the panel. | MUST-HAVE |
| C2 | **`aria-expanded` state on the trigger** | The trigger sets `aria-expanded="true"` when open, `"false"`/omitted when closed; pair with `aria-haspopup`/`aria-controls`. | MDN `aria-expanded` https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/Accessibility/ARIA/Reference/Attributes/aria-expanded · W3C APG Disclosure Navigation https://www.w3.org/WAI/ARIA/apg/patterns/disclosure/examples/disclosure-navigation/ | Tells screen-reader users whether the panel is open — without it the caret is invisible to assistive tech. AA-relevant. | MUST-HAVE |
| C3 | **Active-trail / current-section indication ("you are here")** | Visibly mark the bar item (and, in-panel, the sub-item) for the section the user is currently in — colour/underline/weight change + `aria-current="page"`. | NN/g "Navigation: You Are Here" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/navigation-you-are-here/ · NN/g "Local Navigation Is a Valuable Orientation and Wayfinding Aid" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/local-navigation/ | Orientation/wayfinding — users need to know where they are before deciding where to go. A nav with no current-state is a map with no "you are here" dot. | MUST-HAVE |
| C4 | **Distinct hover AND focus states (not hover-only)** | Every interactive item shows a clear state on hover AND on keyboard focus; focus indicator must be visible (WCAG 2.2 cheap win the project keeps). | Orange a11y "Dropdown menu" https://a11y-guidelines.orange.com/en/articles/dropdown-menu/ · W3C APG Keyboard Interface https://www.w3.org/WAI/ARIA/apg/practices/keyboard-interface/ | Hover-only states strand keyboard users. Visible focus is a stated project baseline (WCAG 2.1 AA + 2.2 focus win). | MUST-HAVE |
| C5 | **Soft hover-highlight to guide the eye within a panel** | Gentle background-tint on the hovered row/column rather than a harsh change. | LogRocket "Here's how I'd design a mega menu" https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/mega-menu-design-examples/ | Guides scanning without visual noise; makes large panels feel calm and navigable. | NICE-TO-HAVE |
| C6 | **Cart/utility badges on the bar** | A count badge (cart, notifications) rendered as an accessible affordance on a bar item. | Baymard homepage & navigation UX https://baymard.com/blog/ecommerce-navigation-best-practice | The restaurant target needs a cart badge; utility affordances belong in the nav system, not bolted on. | SHOULD-HAVE |

## Section D — Header patterns that interact with nav

| # | Pattern / standard | What it is (plain English) | Source | Why it matters | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | **Sticky header — opaque, minimal height** | Header stays pinned on scroll; keep it as short as possible and give it an OPAQUE background distinct from content (never translucent). | NN/g "Sticky Headers: 5 Ways to Make Them Better" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/sticky-headers/ | Keeps nav reachable without eating content. Translucent headers create low-contrast, hard-to-read overlaps. **[fetch-verified: opaque, minimise height]** | SHOULD-HAVE |
| D2 | **Partially-persistent header (hide-on-scroll-down, show-on-scroll-up)** | Header hides as you scroll down (max content), reappears the instant you scroll up. Trigger only after a few px; animate ~300–400ms. | NN/g "Sticky Headers…" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/sticky-headers/ | Maximises reading area while keeping nav one gesture away — the modern default for long pages. **[fetch-verified: 300–400ms, few-px threshold]** | SHOULD-HAVE |
| D3 | **Shrink-on-scroll (condense header after threshold)** | Header reduces height (smaller logo/padding) after the user scrolls past a point. | NN/g "Sticky Headers…" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/sticky-headers/ (minimise height / limit motion) | Recovers space on scroll while keeping the logo+nav present. NN/g caution: prefer NO animation; if you shrink, keep motion quick/smooth. | NICE-TO-HAVE |
| D4 | **Limit / avoid header motion** | Default to no animation; keep the header simply in place. Animate only when shrinking/partial-persist genuinely needs it, and keep it quick. | NN/g "Sticky Headers…" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/sticky-headers/ | Sudden appear/disappear or slow animation on a persistent element is distracting on every scroll. **[fetch-verified]** | MUST-HAVE (restraint) |
| D5 | **Evaluate whether sticky is warranted** | Cost-benefit: only make the header sticky if users genuinely need nav/login/logo throughout the session; otherwise the screen sacrifice isn't justified. | NN/g "Sticky Headers…" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/sticky-headers/ | Prevents sticky-by-default cargo-culting; the framework should let the builder turn it off. | SHOULD-HAVE (as an option) |
| D6 | **Two-row header: utility bar + nav row** | A slim top utility strip (contact, account, language, search) above the main nav row. | Baymard homepage & navigation UX https://baymard.com/blog/ecommerce-navigation-best-practice · Smashing "Efficiently Simplifying Navigation, Part 3" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2014/09/efficiently-simplifying-navigation-interaction-design/ | Exactly the Indus Foods two-row header. Separates secondary/utility links from primary nav so the main bar stays clean. | SHOULD-HAVE |
| D7 | **Search in the nav** | Expose site search in the header (persistent field or expand-on-click icon). | Baymard "Provide a Hover Delay…" https://baymard.com/blog/dropdown-menu-flickering-issue (users fall back to search) · Baymard homepage & navigation UX https://baymard.com/blog/ecommerce-navigation-best-practice | When nav fails or the catalogue is large, users pivot to search — it must be present and reachable from the header. | SHOULD-HAVE |

## Section E — Failure modes to design against

| # | Failure mode | What goes wrong (plain English) | Source | Consequence | Tier (avoid) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| E1 | **Pointer-trapping panel** | Panel opens so fast / closes so slow that it blocks the content the user was actually reaching for while scrolling. | Smashing "User-Friendly Mega-Dropdowns: When Hover Menus Fail" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2021/05/frustrating-design-patterns-mega-dropdown-hover-menus/ | Every slow scroll pops a panel over the page; users feel ambushed. Fix via intent delay (A1/A2). | MUST avoid |
| E2 | **Accidental dismissal (menu runs away)** | Moving toward the panel crosses off the trigger and it closes before arrival (the A4 diagonal problem). | Smashing "…When Hover Menus Fail" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2021/05/frustrating-design-patterns-mega-dropdown-hover-menus/ · NN/g "Timing Guidelines…" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/timing-exposing-content/ | User can't reach the links; nav feels hostile. Fix via close-delay (A3) + safe-triangle (A5). | MUST avoid |
| E3 | **Dead zones / gaps** | Unintended gaps between trigger and panel (or between items) where hover is lost, snapping the panel shut. | Smashing "…When Hover Menus Fail" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2021/05/frustrating-design-patterns-mega-dropdown-hover-menus/ | Same runaway effect; fix by bridging the gap with an invisible hover region or the safe-triangle. | MUST avoid |
| E4 | **Delay-only "fix" that adds lag** | Solving flicker purely with big open/close delays makes every intentional interaction feel sluggish. | Smashing "…When Hover Menus Fail" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2021/05/frustrating-design-patterns-mega-dropdown-hover-menus/ | Frequent nav users notice the lag on every use. Prefer intent-tracking (A5) over long delays. | Avoid |
| E5 | **User has no control over open/close** | Root cause of most complaints: users can't predict when the panel appears/vanishes. | Smashing "…When Hover Menus Fail" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2021/05/frustrating-design-patterns-mega-dropdown-hover-menus/ | Erodes trust in the nav; drives fall-back to search or exit. Give click-open + Esc-close + predictable timing. | MUST avoid |
| E6 | **Decorative-only imagery** | Filling panel space with a generic image that communicates nothing. | NN/g "Mega Menus Gone Wrong" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-gone-wrong/ | Wastes the panel's biggest advantage (visual scent) and enlarges it for no gain. **[fetch-verified: USA.gov]** | Avoid |
| E7 | **Obscured grouping / ambiguous column count** | Separators and lists that make it unclear how many groups exist or what the organising principle is. | NN/g "Mega Menus Gone Wrong" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-gone-wrong/ | Users "assume randomness" and abandon the panel. Fix with clear headings + one item per group. **[fetch-verified: USA.gov / Hulu]** | MUST avoid |
| E8 | **Over-nesting / mega where a dropdown belongs** | Building a two-dimensional mega-panel for a handful of links, or nesting many levels deep. | IxDF "What are Mega Menus?" https://ixdf.org/literature/topics/mega-menus · NN/g "Mega Menus Work Well" https://www.nngroup.com/articles/mega-menus-work-well/ | Intimidating, slower, harder to build/clone than a plain dropdown. Apply the B10 threshold. | Avoid |
| E9 | **Hover-only with no keyboard/touch path** | Panel that only responds to mouse hover. | Toward "Breaking up with hover" https://toward.studio/latest/breaking-up-with-hover-navigation-menus-for-websites · W3C SC 1.4.13 https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/content-on-hover-or-focus.html | Fails WCAG 2.1 AA and breaks on touch laptops. Always provide click/keyboard + Esc + hoverable panel. | MUST avoid |

## Section F — Accessibility architecture (the correct ARIA model)

| # | Pattern / standard | What it is (plain English) | Source | Why it matters | Tier |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | **Use the DISCLOSURE pattern, not the ARIA `menu`/`menubar` role, for site nav** | Site navigation should be a `nav` with links whose expandable sections use the disclosure pattern (button toggles `aria-expanded`), NOT `role="menu"`/`menubar`. The true `menu` role demands app-menu keyboard mechanics (arrow-key composite focus, type-ahead) users don't expect from a website. | W3C APG "Disclosure Navigation Menu" https://www.w3.org/WAI/ARIA/apg/patterns/disclosure/examples/disclosure-navigation/ · W3C APG "Navigation Menubar Example" https://www.w3.org/WAI/ARIA/apg/patterns/menubar/examples/menubar-navigation/ | Choosing the wrong role is the single most common a11y mistake in nav — it makes screen-reader behaviour worse, not better. Disclosure = Tab/Shift+Tab between items, Enter/Space to toggle, Esc to close. | MUST-HAVE |
| F2 | **Semantic base: `<nav>` + list + real links; enhance with JS** | Build on a plain semantic list of anchors that works without JS, then layer the panel behaviour on top (progressive enhancement). | A List Apart "Drop-Down Menus, Horizontal Style" https://alistapart.com/article/horizdropdowns/ · Smashing "Building Accessible Menu Systems" https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2017/11/building-accessible-menu-systems/ | Robust, crawlable, and degrades gracefully — matches the framework's vanilla-JS + no-jQuery constraint and cloning-pipeline reproducibility. | MUST-HAVE |
| F3 | **Keyboard contract: Tab in, Enter/Space toggle, Esc close, focus-visible** | Full keyboard operability with a documented key contract and visible focus throughout the panel. | W3C APG "Developing a Keyboard Interface" https://www.w3.org/WAI/ARIA/apg/practices/keyboard-interface/ · Orange a11y "Dropdown menu" https://a11y-guidelines.orange.com/en/articles/dropdown-menu/ | Without a defined key contract, keyboard users get trapped or can't close the panel. Esc-to-close also satisfies WCAG 1.4.13 "dismissible". | MUST-HAVE |
| F4 | **44px touch/click targets** | Bar items and panel links sized ≥44px hit area. | Orange a11y "Dropdown menu" https://a11y-guidelines.orange.com/en/articles/dropdown-menu/ | Stated project baseline (beats WCAG 2.2's 24px). Prevents mis-taps on touch laptops and helps motor-impaired users. | MUST-HAVE |

## TOP GAPS — what a WP-competitor-only feature set would miss

These are the highest-value things you get from studying the *general web* that Kadence/Spectra/GenerateBlocks/Elementor/Bricks nav builders typically do NOT give you out of the box. Ranked by impact for a best-in-class 2026 nav.

1. **Safe-triangle / intent-cone hover path (A5, G2).** The #1 differentiator. Almost no WP nav builder implements cursor-intent tracking; they rely on crude hover + a fixed delay (or nothing). This single feature is the difference between a mega-menu that feels premium and one that "runs away". Vanilla-JS achievable.

2. **Tuned hover-intent + close-grace timing as first-class attributes (A1–A3).** Expose open-delay (~300–500ms), close-delay (~0.5s), and show/hide speeds as block attributes with sane defaults. WP builders usually hard-code or omit these; getting them right eliminates the flicker that Baymard found on 60% of sites.

3. **Correct ARIA architecture — disclosure pattern, not `role="menu"` (F1).** Most WP nav output picks the wrong a11y model (or none). Shipping the W3C disclosure pattern by default (Tab/Enter/Esc, `aria-expanded`, `aria-current`) is a genuine "exceed the field" move and satisfies the WCAG 2.1 AA baseline structurally.

4. **WCAG 1.4.13 compliance built into the panel (A9, E9).** Dismissible (Esc), hoverable (safe-triangle/gap-bridge), persistent. Hover mega-menus routinely FAIL this AA criterion; baking it in is both a compliance and a quality win competitors miss.

5. **Multiple content-driven panel layouts as templates (B13).** Photo-grid / split-with-aside-CTA / logo-grid / info-box / link-columns — chosen per menu. WP builders mostly give one rigid mega grid; the Indus Foods brief alone needs five layouts. This is required to "reproduce the best navs", not optional.

6. **In-panel featured slot + CTA/aside as structured attributes (B6, B7).** A promoted item and an action block inside the panel, not hacked in with a spare column. Maps directly to both target menus (restaurant featured item; Indus split-with-aside-CTA).

7. **Active-trail / "you are here" state across bar AND panel (C3).** Wayfinding that most builders leave to the theme's generic `.current-menu-item` class. Best-in-class navs highlight the current section in the bar and the current sub-item in the panel.

8. **Partially-persistent + shrink-on-scroll header behaviour with restraint (D2, D3, D4).** Hide-on-scroll-down/show-on-scroll-up and condense-on-scroll, with NN/g's motion restraint and opaque-background rules. WP sticky-header toggles are usually all-or-nothing and often translucent (an NN/g anti-pattern).

9. **Two-row utility+nav header as a supported structure (D6).** Utility strip above the primary bar, as a first-class arrangement rather than two stacked unrelated blocks — required for the Indus header.

10. **Mega-vs-dropdown decision baked into the block model (B10, E8).** A clear rule (and ideally UI guidance) so a builder uses a simple dropdown for a short flat list and only escalates to a mega-panel when items/imagery/sub-levels justify it — preventing the "mega for 4 links" over-build that WP page-builders make easy to do wrong.
